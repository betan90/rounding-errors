## The incident

In August 1948, with the city of Asheville shut down by a polio outbreak, a physician at the local veterans' hospital named Benjamin Sandler took an urgent message to the newspapers: polio was caused by sugar. Children ate more ice cream, soda and sweets in summer, polio peaked in summer, and a decade earlier Sandler had infected rabbits with polio by lowering their blood sugar. He concluded that low blood sugar left the body defenseless, and that cutting sugar and starch could immunize a person within 24 hours. He said so, in the paper, "without reserve."

North Carolina listened. Through the summer of 1949, residents were urged over radio and in print to give up sweets before polio season arrived. Sugar consumption in the state reportedly fell by around 90%. And polio cases fell by roughly the same amount, from 2,498 to 229.

Sandler had his proof. He published a book, *Diet Prevents Polio*, in 1951.

## Who lied

Nobody, and the medical establishment said so at the time. The University of Michigan had already tried to reproduce Sandler's rabbit results in 1941 and failed. The president of the Buncombe County Medical Society, asked about the theory during the 1948 outbreak, called it "absolutely without foundation" and said reducing sweets had never been shown to affect immunity to polio at all.

They were right, for a reason that makes the episode worse. Polio is seasonal, which is exactly why it tracked ice cream. And a severe epidemic year is followed by a mild one almost regardless of what anyone does, because most of the population that could be infected already has been. North Carolina's 91% drop is simply what a bad polio year followed by an ordinary one looks like. The sugar was irrelevant. The two numbers fell together because summer drives both, and 1948 was always going to be worse than 1949.

## The bit continues

The real cause of the early polio epidemics ran almost perfectly backwards from a sugar scare. Better sanitation, the arrival of the flush toilet, removed the mild infant exposure that once conferred lifelong immunity, so children began meeting the virus later, at an age when it paralyzed instead of passing unnoticed. Polio was, in a grim sense, a disease of clean water. Sandler's theory never quite died either: it survives today in anti-sugar and anti-vaccine circles, recycled as a suppressed cure the authorities ignored.
