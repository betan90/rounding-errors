## The incident

Add up the GDP that every Chinese province separately reports, and for years the total came out higher than the GDP the National Bureau of Statistics reported for the whole country. Not close. Not a margin of error. A gap worth trillions of yuan, every year, like the country contained more economy than it had.

The mechanism was well understood inside China before it was outside it. Provincial officials are evaluated and promoted substantially on the growth their region posts, so the incentive at every level of local government points the same direction: up. Li Keqiang, while party chief of Liaoning in 2007 and later China's premier, put it more starkly than most economists put anything: leaked US diplomatic cables reported him telling the American ambassador that the province's GDP figures were essentially "man-made" and therefore unreliable. He was describing his own province, years before he'd have to defend the national number.

## Who lied

Liaoning did, on the record, and China's own institutions were the ones who said so. In January 2017, the province's governor stood in front of the provincial legislature and admitted that economic data from Liaoning's cities and counties had been falsified from 2011 to 2014. Fiscal revenue in one county was reported at 2.4 billion yuan for 2013; a national audit later corrected it to 1.1 billion. Some local figures were overstated by more than 100%.

The consequences were real and are documented by China's own Communist Party disciplinary process: the province's former party secretary, who held the post while the fabrication occurred, was removed from every public position and expelled from the party. [Full case summary via Caixin Global.](https://www.caixinglobal.com/2017-01-18/liaoning-government-admits-false-growth-data-from-2011-14-101046468.html)

Liaoning was not unique, it was just the one that confessed on camera. Within weeks, Inner Mongolia admitted its 2016 industrial output had been overstated by 40%, and a district of Tianjin admitted its GDP was a third smaller than reported. The head of China's National Bureau of Statistics used the word "zero tolerance" the same year and announced the bureau had punished more than ten people in each of fifteen major fraud cases the year before.

## The fix

In 2019, the NBS stopped letting provinces calculate their own GDP and took the job in-house, unifying the accounting method nationally instead of asking 31 separate bureaus, each grading their own province's homework, to report a number their own boss's promotion depended on.

It worked, by the metric the site can actually check: the provincial-sum-versus-national-total gap that used to run in the trillions has since narrowed close to zero. Academic researchers who rebuilt the numbers independently, using data harder for local officials to manipulate, such as tax receipts, found something less flattering underneath the fixed headline gap: national growth from 2008 to 2016 was still overstated by an estimated 1.7 percentage points a year, even after the NBS's own adjustments.
